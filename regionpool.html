Design Challenge :

How can we make carpooling more accessible for public transportation users?

Emphasis :

Cultivating empathy towards users, user research and synthesis, ideating and refining concepts, prototyping ideas, user testing, data analysis, and design implementation

Project Length :

5 weeks

Team Members :

Anthony Altieri & Michael Chin

Role :

Design Lead and User Tester

The Situation

Before ride sharing applications such as Uber and Lyft introduced carpooling options UberPool and Lyft Line, many users struggled to find carpooling companions. Due to high costs and a mindset for green energy, public transportation users reframed from using these applications.

Understand - Empathize

Needfinding with Ride-Sharing App and Public Transportation Users

Goal :

Interviewees preferred public transportation because of the lower costs; however, accessibility (having to walk to the bus station and having limited bus stations) was a main hindrance. Furthermore, ride-sharing apps were too expensive, but convenient for the interviewees. If carpooling is set up better, they would be open to carpooling with strangers.

What we learned :

All of our interviewers expressed concern with the amount of time wasted in traffic and desire to reduce their time spent in traffic. However, everyone had unique ways to mitigate stress in traffic. For example, stretching, smoking, listening to music, etc. However, everyone expressed that being in traffic with a companion greatly reduces their boredom in traffic.

Test : Online A/B Testing

Goal :

Look at the difference in ride requests from a simple bus icon compared to a textbox "Pick Destination".
These two versions were labelled version A and version B respectively.

Outcome :

For our AB Test, we valued any user that successfully requested a ride as a conversion, and any user that did not as a miss. With the p value of 7.879 we accept the null hypothesis theory. This means that neither version A or version B performed better than the other in our tests. Because the two versions are statistically equal, we decided to choose version B, which was the textbox "Pick Destination", as a preferential choice.
